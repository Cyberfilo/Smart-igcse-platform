Two lines are parallel if their gradients are equal; perpendicular if their gradients multiply to \(-1\).

Parallel\(m_{1} = m_{2}\)
Perpendicular\(m_{1} \times m_{2} = -1\)
Perpendicular gradient trick
Flip and change sign: \(m = \tfrac{2}{3}\) → perpendicular \(m = -\tfrac{3}{2}\)
Example (parallel) Line parallel to \(y = 3x - 4\) through \((2, 1)\): same \(m = 3\). \(y - 1 = 3(x - 2)\) → \(y = 3x - 5\).
Example (perpendicular, Extended) Perpendicular to \(y = 2x + 1\) through \((4, 5)\): \(m = -\tfrac{1}{2}\). \(y - 5 = -\tfrac{1}{2}(x - 4)\) → \(y = -\tfrac{1}{2}x + 7\).
Horizontal/vertical special case \(y = k\) (horizontal) is perpendicular to \(x = k\) (vertical). \(m_{1} \times m_{2}\) doesn't apply because vertical \(m\) is undefined.
