(Extended.) Composing two transformations gives a single equivalent one — sometimes surprising.

Composition order
Apply the transformation on the RIGHT first. "Reflection then rotation" \(=\) rotation \(\circ\) reflection.
Common combinations
  • Two reflections in parallel lines \(=\) translation (perpendicular to lines, by \(2 \times\) distance)
  • Two reflections in intersecting lines \(=\) rotation (about intersection, \(2 \times\) angle between them)
  • Enlargement SF \(-1\) \(=\) rotation \(180°\) about the same centre
  • Reflection in \(y = x\) then in \(y = -x\) \(=\) rotation \(180°\) about origin
Invariant points
Points that don't move under the transformation. Mirror line points for reflection; centre for rotation/enlargement.
Example Reflection in \(x\)-axis then reflection in \(y\)-axis \(=\) rotation \(180°\) about origin. Check with \((2, 3) \to (2, -3) \to (-2, -3)\), which is \((2,3)\) rotated \(180°\).
Describe fully "Rotation" alone \(=\) no marks. Must state angle, direction, and centre.
