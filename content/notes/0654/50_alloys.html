An alloy is a mixture of a metal with other elements (usually other metals). Often stronger than the pure metal.

Why are alloys harder?
Different-size atoms distort the regular lattice → layers can't slide as easily → harder, less malleable.
Common alloys
BrassCu + Zn — musical instruments, fittings
BronzeCu + Sn — statues, ships (corrosion-resistant)
SteelFe + C (small %) — construction, tools
Stainless steelFe + Cr + Ni — resists rust, cutlery
SolderSn + Pb — joining electronics
DuraluminAl + Cu + Mg — aircraft (light + strong)
Why steel not iron? Pure iron is soft and bends. Adding 0.1-2% carbon distorts the lattice, making steel much harder and more useful for construction.
Alloy ≠ compound Alloys are mixtures, NOT compounds. No chemical bonding between the elements; ratios can vary.
